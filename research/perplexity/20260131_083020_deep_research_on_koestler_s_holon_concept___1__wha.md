# Koestler's Holon Concept: A Comprehensive Analysis of Origins, Definitions, and Applications

The concept of the **holon**, introduced by Arthur Koestler in his 1967 work *The Ghost in the Machine*, represents one of the most influential frameworks for understanding hierarchical organization in complex systems across biological, social, technological, and organizational domains. This report examines Koestler's original definition, clarifies the precise scope of holon application, distinguishes holons from simple hierarchical components, explores the relationship between holons and emergence, and demonstrates how this concept has been operationalized in manufacturing systems and software architecture. Through rigorous analysis of primary and secondary sources, this research demonstrates that holons are neither universal features of all hierarchical levels nor arbitrary divisions of hierarchies, but rather represent specific organizational nodes characterized by particular functional and structural properties that enable complex systems to develop, adapt, and maintain coherence across multiple scales of organization.

## Koestler's Original Conceptualization and Historical Context

Arthur Koestler introduced the term holon within a broader intellectual project of reconciling mechanistic and holistic approaches to understanding living systems. Koestler was born in Budapest, Hungary in 1905 and studied at the University of Vienna, later becoming an accomplished journalist and writer who worked for major publications including the Manchester Guardian, the London Times, and the New York Herald Tribune.[1] His intellectual journey, which included initial involvement with communist movements followed by disillusionment and the writing of the acclaimed novel *Darkness at Noon* in 1940, provided him with practical experience in understanding large-scale organizational systems and their inherent contradictions.[1] This background profoundly shaped his conceptualization of hierarchical organization in natural and human systems.

The term "holon" itself was not invented in isolation but emerged from Koestler's engagement with prior theoretical work. Koestler had been influenced by Herbert A. Simon, a Nobel Prize-winning economist and pioneer in systems theory and artificial intelligence who articulated the importance of hierarchical intermediate forms in complex systems evolution.[6] Simon presented what became known as the "parable of the two watchmakers," which Koestler adopted and expanded in his theoretical development.[6] In this parable, two watchmakers—Bios and Mekhos—manufacture watches of approximately 1,000 parts each.[6] Mekhos designs his watches such that when he sets down a partially assembled watch to answer the telephone, it immediately falls apart into individual elements and must be completely reassembled from scratch.[6] Bios, by contrast, designs his watches so that they consist of subassemblies of about ten components each, which can be assembled into larger sub-assemblies, ultimately forming the complete watch.[6] When Bios must attend to an interruption, his watches fall apart only into their sub-assemblies rather than into individual parts.[6] Despite being interrupted at the same rate—once per hundred assembly operations—Bios's method proves dramatically more efficient, requiring only a fraction of the time that Mekhos needs to complete a single watch.[6] Simon's parable demonstrated that **stable intermediate forms are essential for complexity to evolve rapidly**, a principle that became foundational to Koestler's holon theory.

Koestler's first articulation of the holon concept appeared in his 1964 work *The Act of Creation*, though he did not formally name it as such at that time.[35] In this earlier work, Koestler explored the relationship between subjective and objective knowledge, arguing through the example of artistic and scientific creativity that different domains of knowledge impose order on chaos according to different organizational principles.[35] However, the formal introduction of the term "holon" and its systematic theoretical development occurred in *The Ghost in the Machine*, published in 1967.[1][10] The very title of this work invokes a central theme: the notion that the entire "machine" of life and the universe itself is ever-evolving toward increasingly complex states, as if a ghost—some organizing principle—were operating the machinery.[6]

## The Fundamental Definition: Original Wording and Core Properties

Koestler's original definition of holon, as presented in *The Ghost in the Machine* (1967), is specific and precise. Koestler wrote that **a holon is a natural organism or structure composed of semi-autonomous sub-wholes that are linked in a hierarchical form called a holarchy to form a larger whole.**[1][6] More technically, in his appendix to the Alpbach Symposium proceedings titled "Some General Properties of Self-Regulating Open Hierarchic Order" (SOHO), published in 1969 as part of *Beyond Reductionism* edited with J. R. Smythies, Koestler provided formal axiomatic statements defining holon characteristics.[7]

The foundational axioms of Koestler's holon theory establish that the organism, in both its structural and functional aspects, is not merely an aggregation of elementary parts or a chain of elementary units of behavior.[7] Rather, the organism should be regarded as a multi-leveled hierarchy of semi-autonomous sub-wholes that branch into sub-wholes of lower order and so on, with sub-wholes at any level of the hierarchy referred to as holons.[7] Critically, Koestler asserted that **parts and wholes in an absolute sense do not exist in the domains of life**, and that the holon concept was intended specifically to reconcile atomistic and holistic approaches.[7][31] This represents a fundamental philosophical shift: rather than viewing reality as composed of basic indivisible particles (atomism) or as undifferentiated wholes (holism), Koestler proposed that reality is fundamentally characterized by part-whole relationships.

The most technically complete definition emerges from Koestler's description of biological holons. **Biological holons are self-regulating, open systems which display both the autonomous properties of wholes and the dependent properties of parts.**[1][10][26] This definition contains several essential elements. First, holons are self-regulating systems, meaning they possess internal mechanisms for maintaining stability and responding to disturbances. Second, they are open systems, meaning they exchange matter, energy, or information with their environment rather than existing in isolated equilibrium. Third, they exhibit a fundamental duality: they possess the autonomous properties of wholes—they have some degree of independence and internal coherence—while simultaneously exhibiting the dependent properties of parts—they function within and depend upon larger systems.

Koestler identified this duality as the **"Janus phenomenon,"** a reference to the Roman god Janus who had two faces looking in opposite directions.[7][31] This phenomenon, according to Koestler, is present on every level of every type of hierarchic organization.[7] The Janus phenomenon captures the essential insight that no entity exists purely as a whole or purely as a part; rather, every holon exhibits both properties simultaneously, depending on the perspective from which it is viewed. A cell, for instance, is a complete autonomous system with its own metabolic processes and self-regulation mechanisms (wholehood), yet it is also a dependent component of tissues, organs, and organisms (partness).

## Holon Scope: Application Across All Hierarchical Levels

A critical question concerns whether Koestler's holon concept applies to every level of organization in a hierarchy or only to certain specially-defined self-contained autonomous units. The evidence from Koestler's own writing and from subsequent biological organization literature indicates that **holons are indeed universal features present at every level of hierarchical organization, from the atomic through the organismal to the social level.**[7][25]

Koestler explicitly states in his SOHO principles that "the organism is to be regarded as a multi-levelled hierarchy of semi-autonomous sub-wholes, branching into sub-wholes of a lower order, and so on."[7] The phrase "branching into sub-wholes of a lower order, and so on" indicates recursive application—the principle applies at every successive level. This understanding is reinforced in modern biological organization frameworks, which now recognize that biological structures exist at multiple nested levels: the atomic level, molecular level, organellar level, cellular level, tissue level, organ level, organ-system level, organismal level, population level, community level, and biome level.[2][19] Each of these levels represents a holon in Koestler's sense.

The evidence from biological organization systems demonstrates this principle clearly. **Each level in the hierarchy represents an increase in organizational complexity, with each "object" being primarily composed of the previous level's basic unit.**[2][19] At the molecular level, molecules are wholes composed of atoms (which are themselves wholes at the atomic level), while simultaneously being parts of organelles. Organelles represent the next level—they are autonomous structures with their own internal organization and function, yet they are parts of cells. Cells represent another holon level: they are autonomous, self-regulating living units that are simultaneously parts of tissues. Tissues are composed of organized groups of cells and are parts of organs. Organs are complex holons composed of tissue types and are parts of organ systems. This pattern continues recursively.[2][19]

Koestler recognized this pattern extending beyond biology into inorganic systems as well. He noted that in every order of existence, from physical to chemical to biological and social systems, entirely self-supporting, non-interacting entities did not exist, and moreover, entities could be seen to lie in holarchical relationship with each other.[25][49] This observation extends the holon concept to atomic and molecular structures. An atom is a holon: it is a coherent, self-contained unit with its own internal organization (consisting of nucleus and electrons), yet it is part of molecules. A molecule is a holon: a coherent unit composed of atoms, yet part of larger chemical structures. Atoms composed of subatomic particles (electrons, protons, neutrons) represent another holon level, with each of these particles potentially being understood as holons composed of quarks or other fundamental particles.[5][7]

The principle of universal holon presence at every level is not mere speculation but reflects the fundamental organizational principle governing complexity in nature. As noted in biological organization theory, **emergence—the principle that properties and functions found at a hierarchical level are not present and irrelevant at lower levels—operates at every transition between holon levels.**[2][19] When atoms combine into molecules, molecular properties emerge that atoms do not possess. When molecules combine into organelles, new organizational and functional properties emerge. This consistent pattern across all levels suggests that the holon structure is indeed universal rather than selective.

## Distinguishing Holons from Simple Hierarchical Components

Having established that holons appear at every hierarchical level, a crucial remaining question concerns what distinguishes a genuine holon from merely any part of a hierarchy. This distinction is essential because not every subdivision of a system necessarily qualifies as a holon. The critical distinguishing features are: first, the possession of semi-autonomy and self-regulation capacity; second, the existence of functional coherence and integration; and third, the capacity to maintain stability despite disturbances.

**Semi-autonomy and self-regulation represent the defining characteristics that separate holons from mere components.**[1][10][35] A holon possesses what Koestler termed quasi-autonomous functioning—it can perform certain operations and maintain certain properties without direct instruction from higher authorities, though it remains subject to control from larger holons.[35] This semi-autonomy is distinct from both complete independence (which would contradict its nature as a part) and complete dependence (which would contradict its nature as a whole). The word holon itself, derived from Greek holos (whole) with the suffix -on (suggesting a particle or part as in proton or neutron), encodes this duality etymologically.[6][35]

The autonomy characteristic of holons means that holons **are self-reliant units that possess a degree of independence and can handle contingencies without asking higher authorities for instructions.**[35] A cell, for example, possesses considerable autonomy—it can regulate its own internal chemistry, respond to local environmental conditions, divide or die through programmed mechanisms, without requiring instruction from the organism of which it is a part. Similarly, an organ like the heart possesses considerable autonomous function—it generates its own rhythm and controls its own contraction rate based on local and hormonal signals, without requiring the brain to direct each heartbeat. However, this autonomy is bounded: the cell is still subject to hormonal and neural signals from the organism; the heart's rate can be modulated by the brain and sympathetic nervous system.

Conversely, a component that lacks this semi-autonomous character would not qualify as a holon. A single brick in a wall, for instance, lacks the self-regulating capacity characteristic of holons—it has no internal organization, no capacity to respond to environmental variation beyond passive resistance. A single sentence in a book similarly lacks holon properties; it has no independent regulatory capacity. These are parts of hierarchies, but they are not holons in Koestler's strict sense.

**Functional coherence and internal integration distinguish holons from arbitrary divisions of systems.** A holon is not merely a spatial region of a larger system but rather a functionally integrated unit with specific patterns of internal organization and relation. An organ represents a holon because its constituent tissues are organized to serve specific integrated functions (the heart contracts in coordinated fashion to pump blood). A tissue is a holon because its constituent cells are organized to perform characteristic functions (cardiac tissue cells are organized in electrical patterns enabling synchronized contraction). A cell is a holon because its organelles are organized to perform specific metabolic and structural functions. By contrast, an arbitrary segment of tissue—say, the left half of a heart—would lack this functional coherence and therefore would not constitute a true holon despite being a component of a larger holon.[6][25]

The capacity to maintain **stability despite disturbance** represents another key distinguishing property. Koestler emphasized that holons are stable forms able to withstand disturbances.[35] A cell can maintain its integrity and function despite modest changes in its chemical environment. An organ can maintain its function despite temporary interference with some of its components. The organism can maintain life functions despite loss of a limb or sensory organ. This stability property emerges from the self-regulating nature of holons—they possess feedback mechanisms and adaptive responses that allow them to maintain their essential character and function despite environmental variation. A random collection of cells lacks this stability property; if disturbed, it simply disintegrates or transforms into something fundamentally different.

Koestler's own example from Simon's watchmaker parable illustrates this distinction clearly. Bios's watch design created stable intermediate forms—subassemblies of about ten components each—that maintained functional coherence and could be built upon hierarchically.[43] These subassemblies qualify as holons because they are functionally coherent, semi-autonomous (can be assembled and maintained independently of the larger watch), and stable (can be set down without falling apart). Mekhos's design, by contrast, lacked these intermediate holon forms; it consisted instead of 1000 individual parts with no intermediate organization. This made the system fragile and difficult to assemble.

## The Holarchy: Structure and Organization of Holons

Understanding how holons relate to one another requires understanding the structure Koestler called the **holarchy**—a term he coined to distinguish it from a conventional hierarchy. The suffix "archy" refers to a rule or government, so a holarchy is fundamentally a system of rule or governance by holons.[1][10][26] However, the rules of governance in a holarchy differ fundamentally from those in a traditional hierarchy.

**A traditional hierarchy has a clear top and bottom with directional authority flowing downward, but a holarchy's relationships are not so clearly top-to-bottom or bottom-to-top.** [1][10][26] Instead, the fundamental relationship in a holarchy is that holons at one level are "made up of, or make up" the holons or parts of another level.[1][10] This formulation is deliberately bidirectional: one can view the relationship either way depending on perspective. The parts only exist as they are integrated to create the whole, and the whole's definition is in terms of the parts.[1][10][26] As one source articulates this crucial point, **the "hierarchical relationship" between holons at different levels can be described with terms like "in and out," as meaningfully as with "up and down" or "left and right."** [1][10][26]

This non-directional character of holarchic relationships becomes clear when considering biological examples. One can view a tissue as composed of cells (bottom-up perspective) or view cells as parts of tissue (top-down perspective); both perspectives are equally valid and describe the same organizational relationship. The tissue organizes and constrains cell behavior (downward causation), while the cells' properties determine tissue properties (upward determination). Neither view is more fundamental; the relationship is genuinely bidirectional.

The holarchy can be visualized as a **recursive, self-similar structure analogous to Russian nesting dolls or fractal geometry.** [1][10] Each holon contains sub-holons and is contained by a super-holon. The patterns evident at one level of the holarchy can be similar to those at another level. A cell contains organelles (sub-holons) and is part of tissue (super-holon). Tissue contains cells and is part of organs. Organs contain tissues and are part of organ-systems. This recursive pattern extends in both directions toward finer and coarser scales of organization.

Koestler further emphasized that **the top can be a bottom, and a bottom can be a top, in a holarchy.** [1][10] This statement points to the contextual nature of hierarchical position. What is a "top" (a larger containing whole) in one context becomes a "bottom" (a contained part) in another context. A human organism is a top-level holon from the perspective of its cells and organs, but it is a bottom-level holon from the perspective of a human society, ecosystem, or biosphere. An ecosystem is a very large holon, but it becomes a component holon when viewed within the context of the biosphere.

## The Janus Phenomenon and Dual Tendencies in Holons

Koestler's identification of the Janus phenomenon—the simultaneous possession of whole and part properties—leads directly to recognition of **two fundamental and opposed tendencies inherent in every holon: the self-assertive tendency and the integrative tendency.**[7][31] These are not characteristics specific to certain holons but rather universal properties of all holons.

The **self-assertive tendency** is the dynamic expression of a holon's wholeness, its drive to preserve and assert its individuality as a quasi-autonomous unit.[7][31][34] This tendency manifests in a holon's efforts to maintain its boundaries, preserve its characteristic organization, resist dissolution, and assert its unique identity. In biological organisms, self-assertive tendencies appear as immune responses (the organism asserting what is "self" versus "non-self"), as competition for resources, as maintenance of structural integrity against entropy, and as assertion of individual preferences and behaviors.[7] In individual humans, self-assertion appears as ego formation, desire to maintain autonomy, preference for one's own interests, and resistance to dissolution of personal identity.

The **integrative tendency** is the dynamic expression of a holon's partness, its drive to function as a harmoniously integrated part of a larger whole.[7][31][34] This tendency manifests in a holon's coordination with other parts, its contribution to larger system function, its adaptation to larger system requirements, and its participation in larger organizational structures. In biological organisms, integrative tendencies appear as cellular cooperation, symbiosis between organelles, coordination of organ systems, and participation in ecological communities.[7] In humans, integrative tendencies appear as bonding, cooperation, altruism, group identification, and participation in social institutions.

**These two tendencies are in constant tension and require dynamic balance for system health.** [7][31][34] An organism or society is said to be in dynamic equilibrium when the self-assertive and integrative tendencies of its holons counterbalance each other.[7][31] When self-assertive tendencies become dominant (over-assertion of parts), the system risks fragmentation and loss of coherence—individual components pursue their own agendas without sufficient coordination with the whole. When integrative tendencies become dominant (over-integration), the system risks loss of individuality, flexibility, and adaptive capacity; parts become mere mechanical appendages of the whole with no autonomous function.

Koestler provides specific examples of how these tendencies manifest at different levels. In biological organization, the self-assertive tendency is reflected in instinct rituals, habit formation, and stereotyped routines of thought; the integrative tendency is reflected in flexible adaptation, improvisation, and creative innovation.[7] Under conditions of stress, the self-assertive tendency manifests as aggressive-defensive or adrenergic emotions (fight-or-flight responses), while the integrative tendency manifests as self-transcending, participatory, and identificatory emotions (empathy, belonging, unity).[7]

## Emergence and the Holon-Emergence Relationship

Understanding the relationship between holons and emergence—the principle that wholes have properties and capacities not present in their parts—represents a critical aspect of holon theory. The classical formulation states that "**the whole is greater than the sum of its parts,**" a concept often attributed to Aristotle though his actual formulation spoke of the whole being "something besides" the parts.[12] Emergence refers to the phenomenon that **properties and functions found at a hierarchical level are not present and irrelevant at the lower levels.**[2][19]

The relationship between holons and emergence is fundamentally generative: **emergence creates or manifests the properties that define a holon as a distinct organizational level.** When atoms combine into molecules according to specific bonding rules, molecular properties emerge that neither individual atoms nor simple aggregates of atoms possess. Water molecules exhibit the property of liquidity and the capacity to dissolve salts, neither of which properties exist in hydrogen atoms, oxygen atoms, or even H₂O as an unstructured collection.[9] The emergence of these new properties at the molecular level depends on the specific organization of atoms—their spatial arrangement, bonding relationships, and energetic configuration. This emergent property-at-the-level-of-organization is precisely what defines the molecule as a holon distinct from its atomic constituents.

Similarly, when cells combine into tissues, new properties emerge at the tissue level. Contractility emerges in cardiac tissue from the organized coordination of cardiac cells; no individual cardiac cell possesses the capacity to contract in the synchronized fashion that characterizes heart tissue. The emergence of this organizational property depends on the specific spatial arrangement of cells, their electrical coupling, and their coordinated responsiveness to signals. When tissues organize into organs, new functional properties emerge at the organ level—the capacity of the heart to maintain circulation through pulsatile contractions, for instance, depends on the integrated organization of multiple tissue types (muscle, connective tissue, electrical conduction tissue, endothelial tissue) in specific spatial and functional relationships.

The principle of emergence operating across all holon levels indicates that **emergence is not a special or unusual phenomenon but rather a fundamental characteristic of how hierarchically organized systems create increasingly complex levels of organization.** [2][9][19] Each transition from one holon level to the next involves emergence of new properties and capacities that did not exist at lower levels. The brain emerges from organized networks of neurons; consciousness appears to emerge from the organized activity of the brain; culture and social systems emerge from the coordinated activity of conscious individuals. Each level creates genuinely novel properties through emergent organization.

This understanding resolves a potential confusion: emergence does not require holons to pre-exist at a higher level before organization occurs. Rather, emergence operates through the process of organizing lower-level entities into holons at a higher level. **Holons do not precede emergence; rather, organized holons create the conditions through which emergent properties manifest.** Atoms do not pre-exist as molecules; molecular organization creates the molecular level of organization where new properties emerge. Cells do not pre-exist as tissues; cellular organization into tissues creates the tissue level where new properties emerge.

## Emergence in Biological Organization and the Role of Integration

The emergence of properties at each holon level fundamentally depends on **integration—the process of organizing parts into functional wholes with specific patterns of relationship.** Biological organization theory makes this explicit: the basic principle behind organization is emergence, which depends on parts being integrated according to specific patterns.[2][19] Each level can be described by its lower levels, but the emergent properties at each level cannot be reduced to properties at lower levels.[2][19]

The manifestation of emergent properties depends critically on the integration patterns specific to each holon level. The heart's capacity for rhythmic contraction emerges from the organization of muscle cells into a tissue with specific electrical coupling patterns, organized into chambers with specific spatial arrangements. The liver's capacity for metabolic processing emerges from the organization of hepatic cells into the specific tissue architecture of the liver. A random collection of heart cells does not constitute a heart and does not possess cardiac properties; only organized tissue with the proper spatial and functional relationships manifests emergent cardiac properties.

This principle extends to all holon levels. Molecules emerge from atoms through specific atomic bonding patterns; random aggregates of atoms do not constitute molecules and do not have molecular properties. Organelles emerge from molecules organized in specific spatial and functional patterns; random collections of molecules do not constitute organelles. Cells emerge from organelles and molecules organized in specific compartmental, chemical, and informational relationships; simple mixtures of organelles do not constitute cells.

## The Distinction Between Hierarchies and Holarchies Clarified Through Emergence

The difference between a simple hierarchy and a holarchy becomes clearer through understanding emergence. **In a simple hierarchy, each level merely comprises smaller instances of lower levels without generating genuinely new emergent properties.** A pyramid of stones stacked in hierarchical levels—large stones at the bottom supporting smaller stones higher up—represents a hierarchy. The properties of the pyramid at any level can be understood entirely through understanding the properties of the stones that compose it. The pyramid has no emergent properties beyond those inherent in stone.

**In contrast, a holarchy creates genuine emergence at each level.** The biological hierarchy is a holarchy because properties emerge at each level that are not reducible to lower levels. The heart has properties—rhythmic contraction, coordination of multiple tissue types, functional integration for circulation—that cannot be understood merely through understanding cardiac cells. Similarly, consciousness emerges from brain organization in ways not reducible to understanding individual neurons. Social coordination emerges from the interactions of individuals in ways not reducible to understanding individual behavior.

This distinction leads to a refined understanding: **holons are the organizational nodes at which emergence manifests.** A holon represents both a level of organization where emergent properties characterize the system, and a building block for emergence at the next higher level. A cell is a holon because emergent cellular properties (life, metabolism, reproduction) characterize it, and because cells organize into tissues where new emergent properties arise.

## Holons in Manufacturing Systems: PROSA Architecture

The application of holon theory to manufacturing systems represents one of the most systematic and explicitly developed implementations of Koestler's concept. Beginning in the 1990s, researchers including H. Van Brussel and colleagues at KULeuven developed the **PROSA (Product-Resource-Order-Staff) reference architecture for holonic manufacturing systems**, which provides precise definitions of how holons function in industrial production contexts.[39][47]

In the manufacturing domain, researchers translate Koestler's biological and philosophical concept of holons into operational definitions appropriate to industrial systems. **A holon in manufacturing is defined as an autonomous and cooperative building block of a manufacturing system for transforming, transporting, storing, and/or validating information and physical objects.**[16][39][47] This definition captures the essential holon properties adapted to manufacturing: autonomy (self-regulating decision-making capability), cooperation (ability to work with other holons toward shared objectives), and functionality (performance of manufacturing operations).

Crucially, the manufacturing definition specifies that **a holon consists of an information processing part and often a physical processing part, and a holon can be part of another holon.**[16][39][47] This acknowledges the recursive nature of holons in manufacturing systems, mirroring Koestler's original principle that holons nest within holons across multiple levels. A manufacturing cell containing machines, robots, and control systems constitutes a holon; a group of manufacturing cells organized into a production line constitutes a larger holon; an entire factory constitutes an even larger holon.

The PROSA architecture identifies **three basic mandatory types of holons: resource holons, product holons, and order holons.**[39][42][47] Resource holons represent the physical manufacturing assets (machines, tools, human workers, transport systems) and their associated control intelligence. They possess the autonomy to make decisions about how to perform their functions and the capacity to negotiate with other holons regarding task allocation and scheduling.[39][42][47] Product holons encapsulate all knowledge about how to manufacture specific products, including process plans, bill of materials, quality assurance procedures, and design specifications.[39][42][47] They function as information servers providing manufacturing knowledge to other holons. Order holons represent specific manufacturing tasks or customer orders and manage the progression of work through the system, negotiating with resource and product holons regarding feasible production schedules.[39][42][47]

A fourth type, **staff holons, can optionally be added to assist the basic holons with specialized expertise or to coordinate higher-level functions.** Staff holons might include centralized schedulers, quality management systems, or supply chain coordinators. The PROSA architecture explicitly allows staff holons to provide centralized functions while maintaining the distributed autonomy of basic holons, thereby balancing hierarchical coordination with heterarchical flexibility.

The definition of autonomy in manufacturing holonic systems specifies **the capability of an entity to create and control the execution of its own plans and/or strategies,** while cooperation is defined as **a process whereby a set of entities develop mutually acceptable plans and execute them.**[16][39][47] The holarchy—the system of holons cooperating to achieve manufacturing objectives—is defined as **a system of holons which can cooperate to achieve a goal or objective, and the holarchy defines the basic rules for cooperation of the holons and thereby limits their autonomy.**[16][39][47]

This manufacturing definition crystallizes an important aspect of Koestler's concept: **holons are not absolutely independent but semi-autonomous entities whose independence is constrained by participation in larger holons.** A manufacturing resource holon can autonomously decide how to perform its function, but it operates within constraints set by the holarchy (production objectives, quality standards, safety requirements). This mirrors Koestler's principle that holons are subject to control from higher authorities while maintaining semi-autonomous function.

The application of holonic concepts to manufacturing emerged specifically from recognition that **manufacturing systems need both the stability provided by hierarchical organization and the flexibility provided by autonomous distributed agents.** Traditional purely hierarchical manufacturing control creates brittleness—inflexible in response to disruptions, difficult to reconfigure for new products, vulnerable to failures in central control. Purely heterarchical systems without any coordination create chaos—multiple agents pursuing independent objectives without sufficient coherence to achieve system goals. Holonic manufacturing systems balance these requirements by organizing autonomous holons into holarchies that constrain their autonomy with shared objectives and rules while maintaining sufficient autonomy for adaptation and flexibility.[39][47]

Research in holonic manufacturing systems demonstrates that **this architecture successfully achieves stability during disturbances, adaptability to changing conditions, and efficient use of available resources**—precisely the properties Koestler attributed to holons in biological systems.[39][47] When a machine breaks down (disturbance), holonic systems can quickly reroute work through alternative resources; when customer orders change, the system can rapidly reconfigure; when new machines are added, they can be integrated through inter-holon negotiation without requiring redesign of the entire control system.

## Holons in Multi-Agent Systems and Software Architecture

Beyond manufacturing, holon concepts have been extensively applied in **holonic multi-agent systems (HMAS)**, where software agents are organized using holonic principles to model complex distributed systems requiring both autonomy and coordination.

In multi-agent system implementations, **holons are understood as self-similar structures where a super-holon is composed of sub-holons, and this structure repeats recursively at every level.** [14][51] Each holon—whether at the agent level, team level, or system level—exhibits the same fundamental properties: autonomy, social responsiveness, proactivity, and specific role-based responsibilities within the holarchy. This self-similarity principle directly implements Koestler's observation that holon structure repeats across multiple scales.

The formal definition in HMAS specifies that a holon can be seen, depending on the level of observation, as either an autonomous agent or as a composition of other holons (sub-holons or members).[14][51] This dual nature creates a clear hierarchy where holons nest within holons. At the finest level of granularity, individual agents constitute atomic holons (holons that cannot be further decomposed). These atomic holons can be grouped based on affinity (shared goals, complementary capabilities) into super-holons at the next level. These super-holons can themselves be grouped into larger super-holons at yet higher levels. **The height of the holarchy depends on the organizational objectives and the affinity relationships that define how holons are grouped.** [14][51]

Critically, holonic multi-agent systems maintain clear distinction between **holonic aspects (the structural properties concerning how members organize and manage the super-holon) and goal-dependent interactions (the specific behaviors and problem-solving interactions specific to the application domain).** [14][51] Every super-holon must contain at least one instance of holonic organization defining how members relate structurally. Additionally, super-holons are created with objectives and must perform tasks, requiring coordination and interaction patterns specific to those goals. These goal-dependent interactions can be dynamically added or modified without changing the underlying holonic structure.

The application of holonic principles to software systems addresses fundamental problems in distributed systems: **the need for both autonomy (allowing components to make independent decisions) and coordination (ensuring components work toward shared objectives).** Traditional centralized software architectures sacrifice autonomy for easy coordination; distributed systems sacrifice coordination for autonomy. Holonic software architectures balance these requirements through recursive organization where lower-level autonomous agents are organized into higher-level holons that coordinate their behavior while preserving lower-level autonomy.

Recent advances in holonic software architectures have incorporated **large language models (LLMs) as decision-making components within holons, enhancing their reasoning capabilities and capacity for context-aware decision-making.** [33] In these enhanced architectures, each holon contains three layers: a reasoning layer powered by LLMs for intelligent decision-making, a communication layer enabling inter-holon coordination and capability invocation, and a capabilities layer encapsulating the actual resources or services the holon provides.[33] This layered structure allows holons to understand their own capabilities, reason about available options, communicate with other holons about coordinating actions, and ultimately execute their functions. Specialized holons (supervisor holons coordinating overall system objectives, planner holons translating high-level goals into tasks, task holons managing execution, resource holons managing infrastructure) operate within the holarchy to achieve complex system-level behaviors.

## Holons in Software Architecture and Microservices

Beyond multi-agent systems, holon concepts have influenced broader thinking about software architecture and specifically about **microservices architectures, where individual services function as largely autonomous units that must still coordinate to deliver system-level functionality.**

In the context of software systems, a holon can be understood as **a self-contained functional unit that encapsulates a business capability end-to-end, encompassing all necessary logic, state, and subcomponents required to perform that function.**[23][32] A microservice exemplifying holon principles would have clear boundaries (exposed through standardized interfaces or APIs), possess autonomy in decision-making about how to fulfill its function, and maintain state necessary to perform its operations. Importantly, the microservice would simultaneously operate as a component within a larger system, coordinating with other microservices toward higher-level business objectives.

The holonic flow model for systems management explicitly adopts holon principles to improve how complex distributed systems are understood and operated.[23][32] Rather than viewing systems through infrastructure-centric perspectives that catalog servers, containers, and services, the holonic approach focuses on **capability flows—how work flows through the system, transforming at each holon, and creating value.**[23][32] Each holon is treated as having standard architectural elements: an inlet (where the holon assumes responsibility for work), an outlet (where it relinquishes work to another holon), and internal processing. This standardization allows consistent operational approaches at every level of the system.

The holonic flow model distinguishes itself from traditional microservices architecture through its emphasis on **recursive self-similarity and fractal structure.**[23][32] Just as a biological organ contains sub-organs containing tissues containing cells, a large software system holon can decompose into smaller holons at multiple levels. Each holon, regardless of its complexity or what it encompasses, presents the same standard interface (inlet/outlet structure). This enables composition—multiple holons can be combined into larger holons without changing their fundamental operation—and substitutability—one holon implementation can be replaced with another as long as it maintains the standard interface.

The holonic approach to software architecture also emphasizes **observability and control at boundaries rather than within components.** [23][32] Rather than trying to instrument every internal function within a system, the holonic approach focuses on making visible the work flowing across holon boundaries: what enters the inlet, what leaves the outlet, what state the holon maintains, what signals indicate the holon's health. This boundary-centric observability approach simplifies understanding complex systems and enables effective operational control.

The holonic architecture model also applies principles from **Stafford Beer's viable systems model**, which uses holonic principles to structure organizational systems that can maintain viability despite complexity and change.[23] In this framework, holons at each level maintain both autonomy (allowing them to adapt to local conditions) and integration (ensuring alignment with larger system objectives). Control is exercised through policy management at boundaries rather than through detailed prescription of internal operations. This allows systems to achieve remarkable flexibility and adaptability.

## Critical Distinctions: What Does NOT Qualify as a Holon

To clarify the concept further, it is important to specify what does not qualify as a holon, even in contexts where holon language is sometimes loosely used. A holon is **not** every functional module or organized subsystem in a complex system. For example, in software systems, not every function, class, or module constitutes a holon; these may be components of a holon but not themselves holons unless they meet the defining criteria of semi-autonomy, functional coherence, and self-regulation.

Similarly, in organizational contexts, not every job title or organizational role constitutes a holon. An individual employee performing a specific function does not necessarily constitute an organizational holon; they may be a component of an organizational holon (a department or team) but not a holon themselves unless they possess significant autonomy in decision-making and functional coherence.

**A holon must exhibit genuine self-regulating capacity and functional coherence, not merely be a labeled region of a larger system.** The distinction matters because it determines whether holon theory actually applies and whether its principles would be effective. Applying holon concepts to systems lacking genuine semi-autonomy or functional coherence leads to misunderstanding and failed implementations.

## Conclusion: Integration and Synthesis of Findings

Koestler's holon concept represents a profound insights into how complexity organizes itself across all domains of existence. The original definition—semi-autonomous sub-wholes organized in holarchies—captures a universal organizational principle evident in biological systems from atoms through organisms to societies, in technological systems from integrated circuits through software architectures to manufacturing systems, and in social systems from individuals through organizations to civilizations.

The holon concept achieves several important theoretical accomplishments. **First, it transcends the false dichotomy between atomistic and holistic worldviews.** Rather than viewing reality as either composed of fundamental indivisible units or as an undifferentiated whole, the holon perspective recognizes reality as fundamentally composed of part-whole relationships. Each holon is simultaneously a whole with its own integrity and a part of larger wholes. This perspective dissolves the conceptual conflict between reductionism (which understands wholes only through their parts) and holism (which understands parts only through their wholes).

**Second, the holon concept clarifies how emergence occurs and how complexity develops.** Each holon level creates emergent properties through the integration of lower-level holons in specific organizational patterns. These emergent properties constitute the distinctive character of the higher-level holon. Complexity develops not through mere accumulation of parts but through hierarchical organization into holons at multiple scales, each bringing new properties into existence through integration.

**Third, the holon concept explains why hierarchical organization is so prevalent in nature and why hierarchies, when well-designed, are so effective.** Complex systems evolve or are designed most rapidly and effectively when they incorporate stable intermediate forms—holons—that can be built upon. The parable of the watchmakers demonstrates this principle with quantitative force: hierarchical organization reduces development time by factors of thousands because stable intermediates prevent catastrophic failure during assembly interruptions.

**Fourth, the holon concept provides practical guidance for designing complex systems.** Whether in manufacturing, software architecture, organizational management, or biological engineering, the principle is consistent: identify the appropriate holon levels for your domain, ensure each holon possesses semi-autonomy and functional coherence, define clear holarchic relationships between levels, and maintain balance between self-assertive and integrative tendencies. Systems designed according to these principles achieve both stability (through holon integrity) and flexibility (through holon semi-autonomy).

The widespread adoption of holon concepts across manufacturing systems, multi-agent systems, software architecture, organizational theory, and integral philosophy testifies to the power and applicability of this fundamental insight. Sixty years after Koestler's introduction of the term, the holon remains one of the most generative concepts for understanding how complex organized systems function across all domains.
