## Response

### Discovery vs. Invention in Theoretical Frameworks

In philosophy of science, **discovery** posits that scientific theories and mathematical truths are found in nature (realist view), while **invention** views them as human constructs (anti-realist or nominalist view). Key debates contrast objective revelation through empirical or logical means with creative fabrication, mediated by positions like Popper's falsificationism (theories conjectured but testable discoveries), Kuhn's paradigms (incommensurable inventions during crises), and Lakatos' research programmes (rational reconstruction balancing both).[1][2]

#### Popper's Falsificationism
Karl Popper argued theories are bold conjectures (inventions) awaiting falsification (discovery of errors), emphasizing demarcation via refutability rather than verification. Science advances by discarding falsified theories, rejecting induction as invention-driven myth.[1] (Note: Search results focus more on critiques; Popper's core is conjectures and refutations as non-inventive logic of discovery.)

#### Kuhn's Paradigms
Thomas Kuhn saw normal science within **paradigms** (shared frameworks) as puzzle-solving, but revolutions involve paradigm shifts via gestalt switches—irrational inventions replacing incommensurable discoveries. Anomalies accumulate until crisis, favoring sociological over logical progress.[1][2]

#### Lakatos' Research Programmes
Imre Lakatos synthesized Popper and Kuhn: programmes have a **hard core** (irrevocable assumptions, akin to discovered truths) protected by expendable **auxiliary hypotheses** (invented adjustments). **Progressive** programmes predict novel facts (theoretically and experimentally); **degenerating** ones ad hoc protect the core without new content, marking bad science. Negative heuristics avoid core threats; positive heuristics guide fruitful inventions. Example: Newton's laws as hard core, replaced rationally, not psychologically. "Philosophy of science without history of science is empty; history of science without philosophy of science is blind."[1][2][3][4]

### Mathematical Platonism vs. Nominalism

**Platonism** (Gödel) holds mathematics discovers eternal, mind-independent **Forms**; Gödel argued intuition accesses objective truths, as in incompleteness theorems revealing inherent limits. **Nominalism** (Quine) treats mathematics as linguistic invention, useful fiction tied to empirical science ("to be is to be the value of a variable"). ### Natural Kinds vs. Nominal Kinds

**Natural kinds** (Kripke, Putnam) are discovered rigid designators with essential, mind-independent properties (e.g., "water" as H₂O, known a posteriori). **Nominal kinds** are conventional inventions without essences. No direct search coverage.

### Richard Boyd's Homeostatic Property Clusters

Boyd's **realist** view: natural kinds cluster via **homeostatic property clusters** (HPCs)—causal mechanisms maintaining co-occurring properties (e.g., biological species), discovered not invented. Supports essentialism without rigid boundaries. (Inferred from philosophy of science context; no explicit search hit.)

### Wigner's Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics

Eugene Wigner marveled at mathematics' **unreasonable effectiveness** in describing physical laws, suggesting discovery of pre-existing structures rather than invention. Aligns with Platonism/structural realism.[1] (Indirect via Lakatos' rational reconstruction.)

### Structural Realism (Worrall)

John Worrall's **structural realism** claims science discovers **structures** (relations, equations) persisting across theory change (e.g., Fresnel's equations in Young's wave optics), not objects. Bridges discovery (structures real) and invention (interpretations change). (Contextual via Lakatos' programmes predicting structures.)
